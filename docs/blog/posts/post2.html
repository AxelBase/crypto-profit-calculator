Understanding Exchange Fees: Maker vs Taker Explained

Understanding Exchange Fees: Maker vs Taker Explained

Published: December 24, 2025

When trading cryptocurrency, one of the easiest ways to improve profitability is understanding and minimizing exchange fees. Most platforms use a maker-taker fee model that rewards liquidity providers and charges more to those who take liquidity. Knowing the difference can save you substantial money over time.

A maker is someone who places a limit order that adds liquidity to the order book. These orders sit waiting to be filled by others. Because makers help create a deeper, more efficient market, exchanges incentivize this behavior with lower fees or even rebates.

How Maker Fees Work

Maker fees are typically the lowest available on an exchange, often ranging from 0.00% to 0.10%. Some platforms offer negative fees, meaning makers actually earn a small rebate for providing liquidity. This structure encourages tighter spreads and better price discovery.

To qualify as a maker, your limit order must not immediately match an existing order. It needs to rest on the book until someone else takes it. This small distinction makes a big difference in cost, especially for frequent traders.

Taker Fees and Their Impact

Takers remove liquidity by filling existing orders, usually through market orders or aggressively priced limit orders that match instantly. Because takers reduce order book depth, exchanges charge higher fees, commonly 0.05% to 0.25%.

While the percentage difference seems small, it compounds dramatically. On a round-trip trade, preferring taker orders over maker orders can double your fee expense. For active traders moving significant volume, this can mean thousands of dollars lost annually.

Practical Strategies to Reduce Fees

The most effective way to lower costs is to use limit orders strategically. Place buy orders slightly below the current ask price and sell orders slightly above the bid. These will usually act as makers while still executing quickly in liquid markets.

Many traders default to market orders for convenience, especially during volatile moves. While this ensures immediate execution, it almost always triggers taker fees. Developing the habit of using limit orders pays for itself quickly.

Some exchanges also offer tiered fee structures based on monthly volume or native token holdings. Combining maker orders with these discounts can drive effective fees near zero for high-volume traders.

Understanding the maker-taker model gives you control over one of your largest controllable expenses. Small changes in order placement behavior often yield outsized improvements in net returns. Professional traders treat fee optimization as a core part of their edge, and retail traders can benefit just as much from the same discipline.

Next time you place a trade, pause for a moment and choose the order type intentionally. The savings will compound over every future trade you make.

Master your fees, and you master a significant portion of your trading costs.
